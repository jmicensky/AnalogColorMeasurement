SLIDE 2 — THE PROBLEM: WHY DOES HARDWARE SOUND BETTER?
--------------------------------------------------------------------
[~60 seconds]

Talk track:
  "When engineers like Bob Clearmountain (Bruce Springsteen, The Rolling Stones),
   Chris Lord-Alge (Green Day, Foo Fighters), or Tchad Blake (The Black Keys, Tom Waits)
   are asked why their records sound a certain way, the answer almost always comes
   back to the same thing — the hardware they run audio through.

   Tape machines, vintage console channels, transformer-coupled preamps, tube compressors.
   These devices don't just pass audio cleanly. They shape it. They add what engineers call
   'color' — a harmonic warmth, a frequency tilt, a gentle squeezing of transients
   that makes music feel more alive than a purely digital chain.

   The problem? That hardware costs tens of thousands of dollars, requires physical studio space,
   and can't travel with you to a laptop session.

   This project asks: can we *measure* exactly what a piece of hardware does to a signal,
   model it mathematically, and deploy that model as a real-time plugin?"

Bullet points on slide:
  • Top engineers regularly run tracks through hardware for "color"
  • The effect is real and measurable: harmonic distortion, frequency shaping, compression
  • Goal: Capture it. Model it. Bring it anywhere.

SLIDE 3 — WHAT IS "COLOR," TECHNICALLY?
--------------------------------------------------------------------
[~45 seconds]

Talk track:
  "Analog color comes from three overlapping physical phenomena:

   First, frequency coloration — transformers and filters emphasize or cut certain
   frequencies in ways that aren't flat.

   Second, harmonic saturation — when you push level into a tube or tape, the output
   is no longer a clean copy of the input. It's slightly bent. That bending
   creates harmonic overtones — typically 2nd and 3rd harmonics — that our ears
   interpret as warmth or presence.

   Third, dynamic behavior — the way a device responds differently at different levels.
   Push harder, and the transfer curve changes. A compressor adds gain reduction.
   A tape saturates more aggressively.

   Together, these three properties form the 'signature' of a device. Our job is to
   measure and model all three."

Bullet points on slide:
  • Frequency coloration (linear filtering)
  • Harmonic saturation (nonlinear transfer curve)
  • Level-dependent behavior (dynamic response)

SLIDE 4 — THE ALGORITHMS: MATHEMATICAL TOOLS
--------------------------------------------------------------------
[~90 seconds]

Talk track:
  "To model these properties, the system uses four core algorithms."

--- Algorithm 1: Wiener FIR (Frequency Response Estimation) ---
  Purpose: Captures the linear frequency coloring of the device.

  Equation:
    H[k] = Σ (conj(X[k]) · Y[k])  /  (Σ |X[k]|² + λ)

    X[k] = reference signal spectrum
    Y[k] = recorded signal spectrum
    λ    = regularization constant (~-60 dB relative)

  Plain English: "We play a known sweep signal through the hardware and record
  what comes back. This equation computes the best-fit linear filter that explains
  the difference — the EQ curve of the device."

--- Algorithm 2: Waveshaper Table ---
  Purpose: Captures the nonlinear transfer curve (saturation/clipping behavior).

  Equation:
    y = f(x)   where f is a 1024-point lookup table,
                built by averaging: output_value vs. input_amplitude
                across multiple sine-tone measurements

  Plain English: "We play sine tones at multiple levels and map each input amplitude
  to the output amplitude the hardware produces. This creates a curve — the device's
  'transfer function' — which we store in a table for real-time playback."

--- Algorithm 3: THD Measurement ---
  Purpose: Quantifies harmonic distortion to characterize saturation character.

  Equation:
    THD% = 100 × √( Σ|Hₙ|² / |H₁|² )   for harmonics n = 2 through 9

  Plain English: "After each sine tone capture, we measure how much energy appeared
  at harmonic frequencies that weren't in the original signal. This tells us
  how 'saturated' a device is at a given level."

--- Algorithm 4: Latency Alignment (Cross-Correlation) ---
  Purpose: Aligns reference and recorded signals in time before analysis.

  Equation:
    delay = argmax_τ { Σ x[n] · y[n + τ] }
    Sub-sample correction via 4-point Lagrange interpolation

  Plain English: "Any audio interface introduces some delay between what we send
  and what we receive. This algorithm finds that delay to sample accuracy and corrects
  for it, so our mathematical comparison is perfectly time-aligned."

SLIDE 6 — THE PROFILER APP (Hardware Profiler)
--------------------------------------------------------------------
[~60 seconds]

Talk track:
  "The profiler is a standalone app you use in your studio.

   You connect your hardware device — a preamp, a compressor, a tape machine —
   to your audio interface using a send/return loop. The profiler routes audio
   out through the hardware and records what comes back.

   You tell the app how many measurements you want — a Quick pass takes 30 seconds
   and plays a frequency sweep plus a 1kHz sine tone. A HiFi pass takes 3 minutes
   and adds pink noise, a low-frequency tone, and a drum loop.

   It automatically corrects for any latency introduced by your interface,
   saves the raw recordings, and then runs all four algorithms to fit the model.
   The result is a single model file that captures that device's unique sound."

Bullet points on slide:
  • Connects to hardware via send/return loop on any audio interface
  • Plays calibrated test signals: sine sweeps, pink noise, drum loops
  • Automatically detects and corrects round-trip latency
  • Exports a model file: frequency response + transfer curve + distortion profile
  • Quick mode: 30 sec | Standard: 2 min | HiFi: 3 min


--------------------------------------------------------------------
SLIDE 7 — THE PLUGIN (Hardware Color)
--------------------------------------------------------------------
[~45 seconds]

Talk track:
  "Once you have a model file, you load it into the plugin — a standard VST3/AU
   that works in any DAW.

   The plugin processes audio in real time through the model in three stages,
   matching the L-N-L architecture: first the linear EQ coloring of the device,
   then the nonlinear saturation, then any post-saturation shaping.

   Three controls give you artistic flexibility:
   Drive scales the input level, controlling how hard the saturation hits —
   just like pushing level into real hardware.
   Weight tilts the tonal balance, warmer or brighter, without breaking the model.
   Mix blends the processed signal with the dry signal for parallel processing.

   The plugin's latency is reported to the DAW so it's automatically compensated."

Bullet points on slide:
  • Loads any model file captured with the profiler
  • L-N-L architecture: linear filter → waveshaper → output filter
  • Drive: controls saturation intensity (like pushing hardware harder)
  • Weight: tonal tilt — warmer or brighter
  • Mix: dry/wet blend for parallel saturation
  • Works in any DAW as VST3 or AU
